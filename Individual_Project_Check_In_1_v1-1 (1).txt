1 PROJECT OVERVIEW
Background & Motivation
In AP CS, free-response questions are more challenging than multiple-choice
questions. Most students have no idea how to start or get stuck at a certain step
because FRQ questions require them to write out their algorithm in a clear, struc-
tured way. In this case, timely and valid feedback is essential for improvement.
In my observation, students in AP classes tend to focus on scores: they rarely
review the feedback report, or they don’t know how to improve for the next
attempt, so they heavily rely on their teacher’s explanations. For instance, many
questions test the counter pattern combined with a reset mechanism. Many stu-
dents don’t know when or how to reset the counter variable to zero, resulting in
a loss of points on FRQs.
Thus, I hope to redesign the FRQ score report not only to explain why students
lose points, but also to guide them on which skills to practice, which questions
test those skills, and how to improve their performance on similar questions.
Ultimately, I want to translate the score report into a practical improvement
plan.
What I am redesigning:
What I am redesigning is the AP Classroom AP CS FRQ student-side score
report interface, where students can see how many points they get and the
corresponding scoring.
Why it matters:
1
Valid feedback can greatly reduce reliance on teachers’ explanations by turning
the reasons for lost points into guidance for next steps. For FRQs that require
consistent reflection and targeted practice, if feedback is not detailed and stu-
dents only focus on their score, their confidence and willingness to practice can
be compromised. Designing the feedback interface to be more actionable can
equip students to improve their performance on similar questions in a more
independent and effective way.
2 DESIGN EXPLORATION (THREE ALTERNATIVES)
Design A: Rubric Breakdown
Design intent:
This design highlights transparency and structure. The goal is to enable students
to see how many points they earned for each scoring criterion, so they can
understand where points are lost.
Key elements:
• Display each scoring criterion
• Explanation for each scoring criterion
• Highlight where points are lost
• Organized information layout
Design B: Anchored Feedback
Design intent:
This design emphasizes a clear mapping between students’ answers and the
scoring rubric, reducing the cognitive load of understanding why points are lost.
Key elements:
• Side-by-side view of students’ answers and comments
• Clear mapping between each comment and the relevant part of the student’s
answer
• Expandable comments that reveal detailed explanations on click/tap
• A consolidated summary of common issue types (frequent mistakes)
2
Design C: Practice Plan
Design intent:
This design emphasizes translating feedback into an actionable practice plan.
Key elements:
• Categorization of common mistake types
• Skill tags aligned with each mistake
• Recommended practice directions or similar questions
• A short “next-step practice plan” section
